11. GALVANOMETER



A Galvanometer is a two terminal measuring instrument capable of detecting the direction as well as magnitude of current flowing through a circuit. It consists of a tightly wound circular copper coil with two terminals, a pointer made up of light weight permanent magnets and a scale. When a current flows through the copper coil, a vertical magnetic field is produced. This magnetic field is either in the upward or downward direction depending on the direction of current. The interaction between the produced magnetic field and the magnetic field of the permanent magnet causes the magnet to experience a force, which causes the pointer to deflect. The direction of deflection of the pointer indicates the polarity of the applied current. The degree of deflection of the pointer is proportional to the current flowing in the coil. The scale is helpful in giving a visual representation of the magnitude of current.

14.LIGHT ACTIVATED SWITCH CIRCUIT USING LDR SENSOR



Light activated switch design is performed on the principle of photoconductivity of the LDR sensor. The main principle of this circuit is to switch ON the light when the LDR is illuminated. The LDR has high resistance value in the dark and the resistance decreases as the intensity of the light increases. The system consists of a LDR, comparator, relay module and lamp. A potential divider network is formed by connecting LDR and a fixed value resistor in series. Voltage across fixed resister is fed to the inverting terminal of the comparator and potentiometer to its non-inverting terminal to generate a reference voltage. The comparator compares the voltages at the reference pin, which is the noninverting pin and the inverting pin of the op amp. When light rays fall on the LDR, resistance value decreases and the voltage between its terminals also decreases. This decrease in voltage causes the voltage at the inverting terminal to increase. Thus, based on light intensity, voltage at inverting terminal varies. If the voltage at the non-inverting pin is greater than the voltage at the inverting pin, the output voltage is low and the transistor will be OFF. If the voltage at the non-inverting pin is less than the voltage at the inverting pin, then the output voltage of the comparator is high and can switch the transistor ON. Thus, when the LDR is illuminated, the transistor is switched ON, thereby activating the relay which results in switching the lights ON.

16.METAL DETECTOR



Metal detector is a device that uses a proximity sensor to detect the presence of metal objects within its range. It is designed for metallic body detection by detecting the variations in high frequency eddy current losses. With an external tuned circuit, it acts as oscillator. The output signal level changes the supply current based on the distance of metallic object. The circuit mainly consists of the LC circuit, proximity sensor TDA0161 IC, LED and a buzzer. The coil L1 and the capacitor C1 connected in parallel, will form the LC circuit. An external resistance R1 is selected based on detection range. At the detector hot point side, the integrated circuit acts like a negative resistor with a value equal to R1. When the object is approaching near, the oscillation stops as the tuned circuit loss resistance (Rp) becomes smaller than R1. As a result, the supply current will be more than 8mA. When the object is far, the oscillation is sustained when Rp is higher than R1. As a result, the supply current will be less than 1mA. Based on the supply current, the voltage across the transistor terminals will vary. When the object is near, transistor will turn ON the LED and also the buzzer.

18. PIN DIODE BASED FIRE SENSOR



A PIN diode-based fire sensor is a circuit that activates an alarm upon fire detection. This type of fire detection circuit overcomes the drawback of traditional thermistor-based fire detection circuit that activates an alarm upon fire detection in the close vicinity. The PIN diode has an intrinsic layer in between the p-type and n-type semiconductor regions of the diode and typically operates under reverse bias. It detects visible and infrared (IR) in the range of 430-1100 nanometres. Thus, visible light and IR from the fire activate the sensor. The circuit consists of PIN diode, op-amp, transistor, counter, a piezo buzzer and LEDs. The op-amp is used to convert the photo current generated in the PIN diode to the corresponding voltage in its output. The non-inverting input is connected to the ground and anode of photodiode, while the inverting input gets photo current from the PIN diode. During normal conditions, photocurrent from PIN diode is low. Under such conditions, the photodiode does not pass current to the op-amp. The output voltage of op-amp is low, as a result the transistors do not get activated, and thus LEDs and buzzer remain OFF. When there is a flame, light falls on the PIN diode, and photocurrent flows through the diode, which is converted to the corresponding output voltage using an op-amp. The output voltage activates transistor T1 causing LED1 to glow. The counter in the circuit has 10 outputs which go high one by one as a result of which LED2 blinks. T2 transistor is connected to one of the counter outputs, and when it activates, LED3 glows, and the buzzer beeps.

25. SOLID STATE TESLA COIL



A Tesla coil is a radio frequency oscillator that drives an air-core resonant transformer to produce high voltages at low currents. It is meant for wireless power transmission. Even though the original Tesla coil involves capacitors and spark gaps in the primary circuit, the Slayer Exciter Tesla coil is its electronic counterpart which utilizes solid-state devices like transistors, thyristors, etc. to drive the primary. Unlike the regular spark gap Tesla coils, it is self-tuning due to the feedback from the secondary coil. When the DC power supply is turned ON, the transistor immediately turns ON, and it acts as a closed switch. This enables the current to flow from the supply through the primary coil. Thus, a changing magnetic field is set up in the primary coil coupled to the secondary coil. Due to this, the secondary coil creates its own magnetic field and emf across its terminals as per Faraday’s Law of Electromagnetic Induction. This emf causes the transistor to turn OFF and act as an open switch. Thus, the primary coil current stops, which in turn causes the secondary to stop producing emf. Thus, the transistor turns back ON due to the power supply, and the whole cycle repeats. This ON/OFF cycle takes place several thousand times a second at the resonant frequency of the secondary coil. The top load acts as a capacitor plate for the secondary and stores charges, producing high voltages and powerful electric fields. These fields are powerful enough to excite the gases present in a fluorescent lamp.

32. 4 BIT DAC USING OPAMP



DAC (Digital to analog converter) takes digital inputs and generates the output which is equivalent to the analog signal. By connecting multiple inputs to the negative terminal of the operational amplifier, the single input circuit is converted into a summing amplifier called as a “summing inverting voltage amplifier” circuit. As the negative feedback created by the feedback resistor, RF biases the inverting input of the op-amp at zero potential, any input signals are effectively electrically isolated from each other with the output being the inverted sum of all the input signals combined. Thus, a summing amplifier in the inverting mode produces the negative sum of any number of input voltages, whereas a non-inverting summing amplifier would produce the positive sum of any number of input voltages.

40. COMMON MODE REJECTION RATIO



The Common-Mode Rejection Ratio (CMRR) indicates the ability of a differential amplifier to suppress signals common to the two inputs. Unwanted signals (noise) appearing with the same polarity on both input lines are ideally cancelled by the differential amplifier as these amplifiers are used as a means of suppressing common-mode signals. Real differential amplifiers used in practice exhibit a very small common-mode gain, while providing a high differential voltage gain. A good measure of the diff-amp’s performance in rejecting undesirable common-mode signals is the ratio of the differential voltage gain (Ad) to the common-mode gain (Acm). This ratio is the CMRR. A very high value of CMRR means that the differential gain (Ad) is high and the common-mode gain Acm is low. To implement and measure CMRR, initially differential gain Ad is measured by giving input to non-inverting terminal only with inverting terminal connected to ground. Then common mode gain Acm is measured by giving a common input signal to both the terminals of opamp. Thus, taking the ratio of Ad to Acm to measure CMRR.

58. VOLTAGE PEAK DETECTOR



The working principle of the circuit is such that, the peak of the input waveform is followed and stored in terms of voltage in the capacitor. By the time on moving further, if the circuit detects a higher peak, the new peak value is stored in the capacitor until it is discharged. The capacitor employed in the circuit is charged through the diode by the applied input signal. The small voltage drop across the diode is ignored and the capacitor is charged up to the highest peak of the applied input signal. Initially the capacitor is charged to voltage Vc. The diode employed in the circuit gets forward biased when the applied input voltage Vin exceeds the capacitor voltage Vc. Thereby allowing the circuit to behave as a voltage follower. The output voltage follows the applied input voltage until Vin is more than Vc. As the input voltage Vin reduces below the value of capacitive voltage Vc, it causes the diode to get reverse biased. In such condition, the capacitor retains the value until the input again exceeds the value stored in the capacitor.

The output of the op-amp is low and the transistor is OFF. If the LASER light is blocked by an intruder from falling on the LDR the resistance of the LDR increases and as a result, the output of the op-amp will be HIGH. This will turn ON the Transistor. The output of the transistor is connected to the Trigger Pin of the 555 Timer IC. If the transistor is ON, the trigger pin gets a short low pulse and as a result, the output of the 555 becomes HIGH. This will activate the alarm by turning ON the buzzer. In order to reset the system a push button is pressed.

90. IMAGE COMPRESSION USING MATLAB



Image compression is a technique used in order to reduce the size of images during storing and processing of image. This helps in decreasing space required to store the images. One of the most used lossy algorithms is JPEG algorithm and it works on Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT). It is a type of Fourier Transform which maps real signals to corresponding values in frequency domain. Initially, RGB image in which each pixel has components of red, green and blue is to be converted to equivalent YCbCr format. This method helps to achieve compression rate somewhere around 60% to 67% based on the size of input image.

92. HARD v/s SOFT SWITCHING USING LTSPICE

Read More

92. HARD v/s SOFT SWITCHING USING LTSPICE



Hard switching involves abrupt and rapid transitions between the ON and OFF states of the switches. When a switch turns ON or OFF, there is a brief period during which both voltage and current are non-zero, resulting in power loss and potential stress on the components. This abrupt transition leads to high switching losses, which can impact the efficiency of the converter. In hard switching, the transitions are not synchronized with the zero-crossing points of the voltage or current waveforms, causing the switches to experience higher levels of voltage and current stress. This can result in increased power dissipation and reduced overall efficiency of the converter circuit. Hard switching effects can be minimized by implementing soft switching techniques. The most common method for implementation of soft switching is done by the use of resonant converters like Zero-Current Switching (ZCS) converters and Zero-Voltage Switching (ZVS) converters. Soft switching methods aim to reduce the switching losses by manipulating the timing of the switch transitions, allowing them to occur when the voltage or current is near zero. This approach helps improve the efficiency of power converters and minimizes stress on the components, contributing to better overall performance.
